This page is a recap of

In this page we assume that the spacetime is fixed to be AdS/dS/flat and gravity is treated as background field. In other words, the EFT expansion is controlled by \(G_{N}\).

In the full quantum gravity, the trivial diffeomorphisms are gauge redandency, and candidates of observables should be in the trivial representation of the gauge group, e.g. matrix elements near the asymptotic boundaries.

Under our assumptions, the correlation functions in the bulk are valid observables, in the sense of perturbative expansion of \(G_{N}\).

GKPW vs. BDHM

The GKPW dictionary states that the bulk and boundary path integrals are equal in the presence of deformations:

\[\begin{equation} Z_{\text{bulk}}[\f_0]=Z_{\text{CFT}}[\f_0] \end{equation}\]

On the bulk side \(\phi_0\) specifies the boundary conditions of the dynamical fields, while in the boundary CFT it provides the source terms of operator deformations of the CFT. The boundary correlation functions can be computed by varying the path integral with respect to the source.

The BDHM dictionary states that the boundary correlation functions can be extracted from the leading behaviour of the bulk ones:

\[\begin{equation} \phi(z,x)=\phi_{0}(x) z^{d-\Delta}+{\op}(x) z^{\Delta}+... \qas z\to 0 \end{equation}\]
\[\begin{equation} \phi(r, t, \hat{x})=\phi_{0}(t,\hat{x}) r^{\Delta-d}+\op(t, \hat{x})r^{-\Delta}+... \qas r \to \infty \end{equation}\]

Here \(\op(t, \hat{x})\) is a local operator in the boundary CFT located at the timelike infinity. The mass-dimension relation is \(\Delta(\Delta-d)=m^2\).

For free theory the equivalence comes from that the bulk-to-boundary propagators are attained by extrapolation of bulk propagators with vanishing factors removed. For interacting theory, the argument is due to [1104.2621v2 Operator Dictionaries and Wave Functions in AdS::CFT and dS::CFT, Daniel Harlow.pdf]

Holographic renormalization: separate the bulk path integral into the UV and IR parts

\[\begin{aligned} Z_{\text{bulk}}[\f_0] & =\int \mathcal{D} \bar{\phi} \int \mathcal{D} \phi|_{z>\ell} e^{-S|_{z>\ell}} \int \mathcal{D} \phi|_{\e<z<\ell} e^{-S|_{\e<z<\ell}} \\ & \equiv \int \mathcal{D} \bar{\phi} \Psi_{I R}[\bar{\phi} ; \ell] \Psi_{U V}[\f_0, \bar{\phi} ; \epsilon, \ell] \end{aligned}\]

Note that \(\Psi_{I R}, \Psi_{U V}\) are path integrals over fields located at \(\ell<z<\infty\) and \(\epsilon<z<\ell\) respectively. The fields are joined by \(\phi(x, \ell)=\bar{\phi}(x)\). The source is \(\phi(x, \epsilon)=\epsilon^{d-\Delta} \f_0(x)\), and the variation of \(\f_0(x)\) only affects the UV path integral.

The equivalence of the two dictionaries requires

\[\begin{equation} \lim _{\ell \rightarrow 0} \int \mathcal{D} \bar{\phi} \Psi_{I R}\left[\frac{\delta}{\delta \f_0\left(x_1\right)} \ldots \frac{\delta}{\delta \f_0\left(x_n\right)} \Psi_{U V}\right]_{\f_0=0}=\left.c^n \lim _{\ell \rightarrow 0} \ell^{-n \Delta} \int \mathcal{D} \bar{\phi} \Psi_{I R} \bar{\phi}\left(x_1\right) \ldots \bar{\phi}\left(x_n\right) \Psi_{U V}\right|_{\f_0=0} \end{equation}\]

in which the limit of \(\e\to 0\) is taken firstly. Hence we need to show

\[\begin{equation} \left.\frac{\delta \Psi_{U V}}{\delta \f_0(x)}\right|_{\f_0=0}=\left.\left(c \ell^{-\Delta} \bar{\phi}(x)+\ldots\right) \Psi_{U V}\right|_{\f_0=0}, \end{equation}\]

Turing on the mass term, the result is

\[\begin{equation} \Psi_{U V}=\exp \left(-\frac{\Delta}{2 \ell^d} \int d^d x\left[\left\{\bar{\phi}^2+\ldots\right\}-\f_0\left\{\frac{2(2 \Delta-d)}{\Delta} \ell^{d-\Delta} \bar{\phi}+\ldots\right\}+O\left(\f_0^2\right)\right]\right) . \end{equation}\]

For other interactions we need to introduce counterterms into the effective action.

The picture is that regarding \(z\) as an auxillary imaginary temporal direction, \(\Psi_{U V}\) is the wave-functional of \(\bar{\f}(x)\). The leading term indicates \(\Psi_{U V}\) obeys a functional Schrodinger equation.

dS case

For the dS case, \(z\) is indeed the temporal direction. Maldacena's dictionary is

\[\begin{equation} Z_{\text{CFT}}[\bar{\f}]=\Psi[\bar{\f}] \end{equation}\]

where the right side is the wave functional with \(\ket{x}\)-like state \(\ket{\bar{\f}}\), where \(\bar{\f}\) is the boundary value of \(\f\) as the source of operator \(\op\).

\[\begin{aligned} \Psi[\phi]=\exp & {\left[\int d x_1 d x_2 \phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) G_2\left(x_1, x_2\right)\right.} \\ & \left.+\int d x_1 d x_2 d x_3 \phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) \phi\left(x_3\right) G_3\left(x_1, x_2, x_3\right)+\ldots\right] \end{aligned}\]

In dS bootstrap, the late-time expansion mimics the extrapolation dictionary

\[\begin{equation} \phi(z,x)=\op_{+}(x) (-z)^{\Delta^{+}}+\op_{-}(x) (-z)^{\Delta^{-}}+... \qas z \rightarrow 0^{-} \end{equation}\]

The bulk massive scalar \(\f\) in the Bunch-Davis vacuum can be expanded in the late-time limit as combinations of fiducial local operators in the future spacelike infinity.

The two "operators" \(\op_{\pm}(x)\) carry the principal series representation and are shadow to each other. The mass-"dimension" relation is \(\Delta(d-\Delta)=m^2\). The two point functions include contact terms

\[\begin{equation} \vev{\op_{\pm}\op_{\pm}}= d^{\pm\pm}\, |x_{12}|^{-2\D_{\pm}} \qquad \vev{\op_{+}\op_{-}}\sim d^{+-}\, \d(x_{12}) \end{equation}\]

Comparison

Now similar to the AdS case

\[\begin{equation} \vev{F[\phi|_\ell]}=\int \mathcal{D} \tilde{\phi} \Psi_{I R}[\tilde{\phi}; \ell] \Psi_{U V}[\tilde{\phi} ; \ell] F[\tilde{\phi}] \end{equation}\]

for dS we have

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle\phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) \ldots \phi\left(x_n\right)\right\rangle=\int D \phi \Psi[\phi] \Psi^*[\phi] \phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) \ldots \phi\left(x_n\right) \end{equation}\]

As wave-functions, the phase part (c.f. the free part in the AdS case) of \(\Psi\) does not contribute, and the result is

\[\begin{equation} \Psi[\tilde{\phi}, T]=\exp \left[i S_{\text {local }}+c_\delta(-i T)^{2(\delta-d)} \int \frac{d^d k}{(2 \pi)^d} \tilde{\phi}_{-\vec{k}} \tilde{\phi}_{\vec{k}}\left\{k^{2 \delta-d}+\ldots\right\}\right] \end{equation}\]
\[\begin{equation} \mathcal{Z}_T[J]=\exp \left[\frac{2(-T)^d}{c_\delta \cos (\pi(\delta-d))} \int \frac{d^d k}{(2 \pi)^d} J_{-\vec{k}} J_{\vec{k}}\left\{(-k T)^{d-2 \delta}+\ldots\right\}\right] \end{equation}\]

The distinction between \(\mathrm{dS}\) and AdS that we found in the last section boiled down to the presence for AdS of local terms of the form \(e^{-\frac{1}{\ell^d} \int d^d x \tilde{\phi}^2}\) in the probability distribution for bulk expectation values of \(\tilde{\phi}\). Such terms are sharply peaked around \(\tilde{\phi}=0\), approaching a \(\delta\)-function as \(\ell \rightarrow 0\). They are the manifestation of the "fixed" nature of the AdS boundary conditions, where we set the coefficient of the "non-normalizable" mode to zero by hand. In dS these "peaked" terms cancel between \(\Psi\) and \(\Psi^*\) and are not present in the probability distribution. This happens because in dS space we fixed the initial state of the system at some earlier time, while at the future boundary both modes are allowed to fluctuate. The late-time correlator is then dominated by the mode that falls off more slowly.

  • In Euclidean \(A d S_{d+1}\), either the differentiation of the partition function with respect to boundary conditions or the extrapolation of bulk correlators of \(\phi\) to the boundary produce CFT correlators of an operator of dimension \(\Delta=\frac{d}{2}+\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{d^2+4 m^2}\).
  • In Lorentzian \(d S_{d+1}\), the extrapolated bulk two-point functions are a sum of two terms. One of these has the leading behavior of a CFT correlator of an operator of dimension \(d-\delta=\frac{d}{2}-\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{d^2-4 m^2}\), while the other has leading behavior charateristic of \(\delta=\) \(\frac{d}{2}+\frac{1}{2} \sqrt{d^2-4 m^2}\)

  • In Lorentzian \(d S_{d+1}\), functional derivatives ofl the late-time Schrodinger wave-function produce CFT correlators only of dimension \(\delta\).

  • In the AdS case, the dominant term corresponds to delta source on the boundary, while in the dS case, the two shadow terms are oscillating and get canceled by \(\Psi\Psi^{*}\).

Double-trace deformation?

The wave function

\[\begin{equation} \left\langle\phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) \ldots \phi\left(x_n\right)\right\rangle=\int D \phi \Psi[\phi] \Psi^*[\phi] \phi\left(x_1\right) \phi\left(x_2\right) \ldots \phi\left(x_n\right) \end{equation}\]

can be interpreted as the vev of two CFTs coupled by sharing the common source \(\f\).

\[\begin{equation} \mathrm{CFT}_C \stackrel{?}{=} \mathrm{CFT}_{\Psi} \times \mathrm{CFT}_{\tilde{\Psi}}+\sum_i c_i \mathcal{O}_i \tilde{\mathcal{O}}_i \end{equation}\]

Reconstruction

In AdS/CFT, the bulk operator can be reconstructed by smearing with the bulk-boundary propagator

\[\begin{equation} \f(X)=c\inttt{d'P}{X\cdot P>0}K(X,P)\op(P) \end{equation}\]

Solving the EOM by expanding into eigenmodes renders

\[ \phi(\tau, \rho, \Omega)=\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} \sum_{l, m} a_{n l} \mathscr{Y}_l(\Omega) e^{-i(2 n+l+\Delta) \tau} \cos ^{\Delta} \rho \sin ^l \rho P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, l+\frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(-\cos 2 \rho)+\text { c.c. } \]

with \(\mathscr{Y}_l\) the \(l\) 'th spherical harmonic and \(P_n^{(\alpha, \beta)}\) the Jacobi polynomial. In the case of \(d=2\) this becomes

\[ \phi(\tau, \rho, \Omega)=\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} \sum_{l, m} a_{n l} e^{i l \varphi} e^{-i(2 n+l+\Delta) \tau} \cos ^{\Delta} \rho \sin ^l \rho P_n^{(\Delta-1, l)}(-\cos 2 \rho)+\text { c.c. } \]

In the absence of sources, the boundary value of the field is

\[ \left\langle O_{\Delta}(\tau, \Omega)\right\rangle=\tilde{\phi}(\tau, \Omega)=\lim _{\rho \rightarrow \frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{\phi(\tau, \rho, \Omega)}{\cos ^{\Delta} \rho} . \]

Notice that instead of working with operators, we are working with classical fields. we could lift the boundary condition to an operator equation by dropping expectation values and restoring the hats on \(O_{\Delta}\) and \(\phi .^4\) Let us first reconstruct the operator at the center of AdS. Because the field solution in the origin should be spherically symmetric only the \(l=0\) mode can contribute and the solution reduces to

\[ \phi(\tau, \rho=0, \Omega)=\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} a_n e^{-i(2 n+\Delta) \tau} P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(-1)+\text { c.c. } \]

The \(l=0\) mode part of the boundary field on the other hand equals

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{\phi}^s(\tau, \Omega) & =\sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} a_n e^{-i(2 n+\Delta) \tau} P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(+1)+\text { c.c. }, \\ & =\tilde{\phi}_{+}^s(\tau)+\tilde{\phi}_{-}^s(\tau) \end{aligned} \]

when explicitly splitting up the field into positive and negative frequency modes. This relation can be solved for the coefficients \(a_n\) :

\[ a_n=\frac{1}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right) P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(+1)} \int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{+\frac{\pi}{2}} d \tau \int d \Omega \sqrt{g_{\Omega}} e^{i(2 n+\Delta) \tau} \tilde{\phi}_{+}^s(\tau, \Omega) \]

and plugging back into (3.7) results in

\[ \begin{gathered} \phi\left(\tau^{\prime}, \rho=0, \Omega^{\prime}\right)=\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} d \tau \int d \Omega K_{+}\left(\tau, \Omega ; \tau^{\prime}, \rho=0, \Omega^{\prime}\right) \tilde{\phi}_{+}^s(\tau, \Omega)+\text { c.c }, \\ \text { with } \quad K_{+}=\frac{1}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right)} \sum_{n=0}^{+\infty} e^{i(2 n+\Delta) \tau} \frac{P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(-1)}{P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(+1)} . \end{gathered} \]

The Jacobi polynomials reduce to simple Gamma functions

\[ \begin{aligned} & P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(-1)=\frac{(-1)^n(d+1)_n}{n !}, \\ & P_n^{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2}-1\right)}(+1)=\frac{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right)_n}{n !}, \end{aligned} \]

with \((x)_n=\Gamma(x+n) / \Gamma(x)\) the Pochhammer symbol. The smearing function becomes a sum over \(n\) with Gamma function coefficients forming the standard hypergeometric series, and can be easily resummed to

\[ K_{+}=\frac{e^{i \Delta \tau}}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right)}{ }_2 F_1\left(1, \frac{d}{2} ; \Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1,-e^{2 i \tau}\right) . \]

For even dimensional AdS (i.e. odd \(d\) ) and with the aid of hypergeometric identities, the smearing function becomes

\[ \begin{aligned} K_{+}= & \frac{e^{i \Delta \tau}}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right)}\left\{\frac{\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}\right) \Gamma\left(\frac{d}{2}-1\right)}{\Gamma\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)} e^{-2 i \tau}{ }_2 F_1\left(1,1+\frac{d}{2}-\Delta, 2-\frac{\Delta}{2},-e^{-2 i \tau}\right)\right. \\ & \left.+\frac{\Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right) \Gamma\left(1-\frac{d}{2}\right)}{\Gamma(\Delta-d+1)} e^{-i d \tau}{ }_2 F_1\left(d-\Delta, \frac{d}{2}, \frac{d}{2},-e^{-2 i \tau}\right)\right\} . \end{aligned} \]

The above identities cannot be used for even \(d\) because of singularities that apppear in the Gamma functions. \(K_{+}\)is the smearing function by which an operator at the center is reconstructed using positive frequency modes. The negative frequency modes then automatically appear in the complex conjugated part. Reality of the bulk field then requires \(K_{+}^*=K_{-}\)with \(K_{-}\)the smearing function when we would have used negative frequency modes to express the coefficients \(a_n^*\). Notice that the range of integration in (3.11) is over all boundary operators that are spacelike separated from the bulk operator at the center.

The smearing function is actually more of a distribution, it is only defined when integrated against the boundary operators. Said otherwise, \(K_{+}\)is not unique and can be shifted by terms which vanish when integrated against the boundary operators and

\[ K_{+} \rightarrow K_{+}+e^{i \Delta \tau} \sum_{n=1}^{+\infty} c_n e^{2 i n \tau} \]

for any coefficient \(c_n\). Using this gauge freedom to fix \(K_{+}\)the first term can be canceled and one is left with

\[ K_{+}=\frac{\Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right) \Gamma\left(1-\frac{d}{2}\right)}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right) \Gamma(\Delta-d+1)}(2 \cos \tau)^{\Delta-d} \]

This is a real expression so since \(K_{-}=K_{+}^*\) we have that \(K=K_{+}=K_{-}\). For a bulk point at the origin \(\cos \tau=\sigma \cos \rho\) with \(\sigma\) the invariant distance function between the bulk and boundary point. This allows a simple extension to determine the smearing function for any bulk point. Anti- de Sitter space can be viewed as the goup manifold of the AdS isometry group \(S O(d, 2)\). Applying an isometry will generically map the bulk point at the origin to another point of AdS. Because of the isometry covariance of \(\mathrm{AdS}\), the smearing function can only be a function of the invariant distance 5 between the respective bulk and boundary point. We arrive at

\[ K=\frac{\Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right) \Gamma\left(1-\frac{d}{2}\right)}{\pi \operatorname{Vol}\left(S^{d-1}\right) \Gamma(\Delta-d+1)}(2 \sigma \cos \rho)^{\Delta-d} \]

for any bulk point. A similar procedure can be followed for even \(d\). The resulting smearing function in that case is

\[ K=\frac{(-1)^{\frac{d-2}{2}} \Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right)}{2 \pi^{1+\frac{d}{2}} \Gamma(\Delta-d+1)} \lim _{\rho \rightarrow \frac{\pi}{2}}(2 \sigma \cos \rho)^{\Delta-d} \log (\sigma \cos \rho) . \]

The smearing function has support on the boundary points that are spacelike separated from the bulk insertion point. It cannot have support on timelike separated points because the CFT operators on timelike separated points generically do not commute with the bulk operator. This is basically stating causality.

dS case

In dS bootstrap, the all-order late-time expansion is

\[\begin{equation} \phi(z, x) =\sum_{k=\pm} c_{k}(-z)^{\Delta_k}{ }_0 F_1\left(\Delta_k-\frac{d}{2}+1, \frac{1}{4} z^2 \partial_x^2\right) \mathcal{O}_k(x) \end{equation}\]

Now we look at how a local scalar operator with mass \(m^2>\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2\) in de Sitter space is constructed with a CFT located at the boundary. In the AdS construction the boundary is timelike, and the extra direction emerges from the boundary as a spatial direction. In de Sitter space, the boundaries are located at future and past infinity, which are spacelike boundaries, so what emerges from the CFT is the bulk time. From the boundary point of view the bulk time \(\eta\) appears as a parameter in the definition of non-local CFT operators. As we will see, a local bulk operator that is far from the boundary will be highly non-local from the CFT point of view.

According to the reasoning in the section above, a causal operator should have both components, schematically:

\[ \Phi(\eta \rightarrow 0) \sim A \eta^{\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{+}+B \eta^{d-\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{-} \]

With a certain linear combination, one can reproduce the Wightman function in the Euclidean vacuum. To construct the bulk operator, we evolve the initial data at \(\mathcal{I}^{-}\)with the retarded Green's function, which is

\[ \left.G_{r e t}\right|_{\eta^{\prime} \rightarrow 0} \approx c_{\Delta, d}(-\sigma-i \epsilon)^{\Delta-d}+c_{\Delta, d}^*(-\sigma-i \epsilon)^{-\Delta}-\text { c.c. } \]

in the limit that \(\eta^{\prime} \rightarrow 0\). Here

\[ \sigma=\frac{\eta^2+\eta^{\prime 2}-\left(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right)^2}{2 \eta \eta^{\prime}} \]

is a de Sitter invariant distance and

\[ c_{\Delta, d}=\frac{\Gamma(2 \Delta-d) \Gamma(d-\Delta)}{2^{\Delta-d}(4 \pi)^{\frac{d+1}{2}} \Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d-1}{2}\right)} \]

The bulk operator is constructed by evolving an operator near the boundary:

\[ \Phi(\eta, \mathbf{x})=\int_{\left|\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right|<\eta} d^d x^{\prime}\left(\frac{1}{\eta^{\prime}}\right)^{d-1}\left(G_{r e t}\left(\eta, \mathbf{x} ; \eta^{\prime}, \mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right) \partial_{\eta^{\prime}} \Phi\left(\eta^{\prime}, \mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right)-\Phi\left(\eta^{\prime}, \mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right) \partial_{\eta^{\prime}} G_{r e t}\left(\eta, \mathbf{x} ; \eta^{\prime}, \mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right)\right) \]

where

\[ \Phi\left(\eta^{\prime}, \mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right) \sim A\left(\eta^{\prime}\right)^{\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{+}\left(\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right)+B\left(\eta^{\prime}\right)^{d-\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{-}\left(\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right) \]

By keeping both sets of operators, we are keeping both the positive and negative frequency parts of the solution \({ }^3\). Evaluating the integrand, we have the local operator in de Sitter space expressed as

\[ \Phi(\eta, \mathbf{x})=A_{\Delta, d} \int_{\left|\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right|<\eta} d^d x^{\prime}\left(\frac{\eta^2-\mathbf{x}^{\prime 2}}{\eta}\right)^{\Delta-d} \mathcal{O}_{+}\left(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right)+B_{\Delta, d} \int_{\left|\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right|<\eta} d^d x^{\prime}\left(\frac{\eta^2-\mathbf{x}^2}{\eta}\right)^{-\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{-}\left(\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{x}^{\prime}\right) \]

prescription for a dimension \(\Delta\) operator which reduces to the flat de Sitter space smearing function is

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi(\tau, \Omega) & =\frac{2^{\Delta-d} \Gamma\left(\Delta-\frac{d}{2}+1\right)}{\pi^{\frac{d}{2}} \Gamma(\Delta-d+1)} \int d \Omega^{\prime}\left(\frac{\cos \left(\Omega-\Omega^{\prime}\right) \mp \sin \tau}{\cos \tau}\right)^{\Delta-d} \mathcal{O}_{+}\left(\Omega^{\prime}\right) \\ & +\frac{2^{-\Delta} \Gamma\left(\frac{d}{2}-\Delta+1\right)}{\pi^{\frac{d}{2}} \Gamma(1-\Delta)} \int d \Omega^{\prime}\left(\frac{\cos \left(\Omega-\Omega^{\prime}\right) \mp \sin \tau}{\cos \tau}\right)^{-\Delta} \mathcal{O}_{-}\left(\Omega^{\prime}\right) \end{aligned} \]

respectively for \(\mathcal{I}^{ \pm}\)smearing, with integration inside the bulk lightcone region. This will reproduce the Wightman function in the Euclidean vacuum, expressed in global coordinates.

Now we see that in anti-de Sitter space, with the embedding coordinates, we can write down the smearing function in a very simple and manifestly AdS-invariant way: $$ \Phi(X)=A_{\Delta, d} \int_{\partial A d S} d P(-2 P \cdot X)^{\Delta-d} \Theta(-P \cdot X) \mathcal{O}(P) $$

Therefore for a scalar with \(m^2>\left(\frac{d}{2}\right)^2\) we have the smearing prescription in the embedding space: $$ \Phi(X)=A_{\Delta, d} \int_{\partial d S} d P(2 P \cdot X)^{\Delta-d} \Theta(P \cdot X) \mathcal{O}{+}(P)+B \int_{\partial d S} d P(2 P \cdot X)^{-\Delta} \Theta(P \cdot X) \mathcal{O}_{-}(P) $$-*

Flat spacetime

BMS4 algebra

\[\begin{aligned} & {\left[L_n, L_m\right]=(n-m) L_{n+m}, \quad\left[\bar{L}_n, \bar{L}_m\right]=(n-m) \bar{L}_{n+m}} \\ & {\left[L_n, M_{r, s}\right]=\left(\frac{n+1}{2}-r\right) M_{n+r, s}, \quad\left[\bar{L}_n, M_{r, s}\right]=\left(\frac{n+1}{2}-s\right) M_{r, n+s}} \\ & {\left[M_{p, q}, M_{r, s}\right]=0} \end{aligned}\]
\[\begin{align} &L_0=\frac{J_3-i K_3}{2}, \quad L_{-1}=\frac{-J_1+i J_2+i K_1+K_2}{2}, \quad L_1=\frac{J_1+i J_2-i K_1+K_2}{2} \\ &\bar{L}_0=\frac{-J_3-i K_3}{2}, \quad \bar{L}_{-1}=\frac{J_1+i J_2+i K_1-K_2}{2}, \quad \bar{L}_1=\frac{-J_1+i J_2-i K_1-K_2}{2} \end{align}\]

Poincare basis

\[\begin{align} &\tilde{J}_{i j}=J_{i j}, \quad \tilde{J}_{i 0}=-\frac{1}{2}\left(P_i+K_i\right), \quad \tilde{J}_{i d}=\frac{1}{2}\left(P_i-K_i\right), \quad \tilde{J}_{0 d}=-D \\ &\tilde{P}_0=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(H+K), \quad \tilde{P}_i=-\sqrt{2} B_i, \quad \tilde{P}_d=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(K-H) \end{align}\]

Carrollian conformal basis

\[\begin{align} & D=L_0+\bar{L}_0, \quad J^{12}=-i\left(L_0-\bar{L}_0\right), \\ & B^1=-\frac{1}{2}\left(M_{0,1}+M_{1,0}\right), \quad B^2=\frac{i}{2}\left(M_{0,1}-M_{1,0}\right), \\ & P^0=-M_{0,0}, \quad P^1=L_{-1}+\bar{L}_{-1}, \quad P^2=-i\left(L_{-1}-\bar{L}_{-1}\right), \\ & K^0=M_{1,1}, \quad K^1=L_1+\bar{L}_1, \quad K^2=i\left(L_{-1}-\bar{L}_{-1}\right) . \end{align}\]

Representations

Bruhat-like decomposition: \(\glie=\lie{m}\os \lie{a} \os \lie{n} \os \bar{\lie{n}}\)

\[\begin{align} & {[D, P]=p_D P, \quad[D, K]=k_D K} \\ & {[M, P]=p_M P, \quad[M, K]=k_M P, \quad[P, K]=d D+m M} \end{align}\]

The rotation-part is \(\lie{m}=\set{J^{12},B^{1},B^{2}}\)

Remarks:

  • Choosing an origin or inserting an operator to the celestial sphere means selecting a special null direction \(z\), hence special plane \(t-z\) in the bulk.

  • The BPZ-like conjugation of the Carrollian basis seemingly requires a double Wick rotation.

  • The anti-Hermiticity in the Poincare basis implies the Carrollian basis is also anti-Hermitian, and in the conformal basis gives

\[\begin{equation} L_{n}^{\dagger}=-\bar{L}_{n} \end{equation}\]

  • The nontrivial action of \(B\) in finite-length multiplets means finite derivative operators in the transverse directions. But \(P^{0}\) (and \(K^{0}\)) generates infinitely many derivative operators. Together this implies:

    • the infinite-length multiplets are more symmetric with respect to the Poincare basis, and unitary reps can arise in this way (c.f. the construction of principal series representations);

    • the finite-length multiplets must break the translation symmetries in the bulk.

The Bondi cooridnates of \(\RR^{d-1,1}\) are \((u,r,\hat{x})\) where \(u\) is the retarded time, \(r\) is the radius, and the hat notation \(\hat{x}\in\sphere{d-2}\) denotes the spherical coordinates of \(\sphere{d-2}\) embedded into \(\RR^{d-1}\). The metric is

\[\begin{equation} d s^2=-d u^2-2 d u d r+r^2 ds^{2}_{\sphere{d-2}} \end{equation}\]

For massless free scalar, the leading order of the near-infinity expansion is

\[\begin{equation} \phi(r,u,\hat{x})=\op(u,\hat{x}) r^{-\Delta}+... \qas r \rightarrow \oo \end{equation}\]

and the operator \(\op(u,\hat{x})\) carries the singlet scalar representation with \(\D=\frac{d-2}{2}\) in Carrollian CFT. The two-point function is (upto possible \(i\e\)-prescription)

\[\begin{equation} \vev{\op\op}=d\, |\hat{x}_{12}|^{-d+2} \end{equation}\]

The bulk field can be reconstructed as

\[\begin{equation} \phi_I^{(s)}(X)=-\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int d^2 w \varepsilon_I^{*+}(w, \bar{w}) \partial_{\tilde{u}} \bar{\phi}_{z \ldots z}^{(s)}\left(\tilde{u}=-q^\mu X_\mu, w, \bar{w}\right)+\text { h.c. } \end{equation}\]

The bulk-to-boundary propagator is

\[\begin{equation} \partial_{u} \delta\left(q^\mu X_\mu+u\right) \end{equation}\]
